Antenna 101 · Learning center

The antenna is the most important part of your radio.

You can spend $5 000 on a transceiver and still get worked by a $50 radio with a better antenna. This guide explains why, how to measure what you have (SWR, return loss, resonance), and how to actually tune an antenna instead of just hoping it works.

1 · Antenna fundamentals

What an antenna actually is, and why it matters more than your radio.

An antenna is a transducer between guided waves on a transmission line and free-space electromagnetic waves. On transmit, RF current oscillates along a conductor and radiates an electromagnetic wave. On receive, an arriving wave induces a tiny RF voltage in the same conductor.

Three things determine how well an antenna works:

  • Resonance — is its electrical length the right fraction of a wavelength at your operating frequency?
  • Impedance match — does it present 50 Ω (or close) to your feedline so power transfers instead of reflecting back?
  • Pattern & gain — where does it radiate (overhead, low-angle, omnidirectional, beamed)?
A "10 dB better antenna" beats "10 dB more transmit power" for the same money — and it improves your receive too. Power amps don't help you hear weak signals; antennas do.

Polarization matters

The orientation of the radiating element sets polarization. A vertical whip is vertically polarized; a horizontal dipole is horizontally polarized. Cross-polarization (e.g. vertical TX into a horizontal RX antenna) costs you ~20 dB at VHF/UHF line-of-sight. HF skywave scrambles polarization, so it matters less there.

2 · Wavelength & resonance

Why antennas care about frequency, and how to compute physical lengths.

An antenna is resonant at the frequency where its electrical length equals an integer number of half-wavelengths (or quarter-wavelengths against a ground plane). At resonance the reactance (the imaginary part of its impedance) is zero — the antenna looks purely resistive to your feedline, and current flows efficiently.

λ (m) = 300 / f (MHz) ½ λ (m) = 150 / f (MHz) ¼ λ (m) = 75 / f (MHz) Actual wire is shorter: L = (¼λ) × velocity-factor (~0.95 for thin wire in air)

Why shorter? Real conductors have end-effects, capacitance to nearby objects, and finite thickness. The classic ham rule of thumb for a half-wave dipole in feet is:

L_ft = 468 / f (MHz) (each leg = L_ft / 2)
Resonant length calculatorvelocity factor 0.95
A non-resonant antenna isn't broken — it just needs a tuner / matching network to present 50 Ω to your radio. Resonance and SWR are related but not the same thing (see § 3 and § 4).

3 · Understanding SWR (standing-wave ratio)

SWR is a symptom, not a disease. It tells you how much power is reflected — and that's it.

SWR is the ratio of maximum to minimum voltage on a mismatched transmission line. A perfectly matched 50 Ω antenna gives SWR = 1.0:1 (zero reflection). A complete short or open gives SWR = ∞ (all reflected).

|Γ| = (Z_load − Z₀) / (Z_load + Z₀) (reflection coefficient) SWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 − |Γ|) RL = −20 · log₁₀(|Γ|) dB (return loss) P_refl / P_fwd = |Γ|² = ((SWR−1)/(SWR+1))²

What SWR actually costs you

The forward power that bounces back at the antenna travels back down the coax, through your radio, and into the dummy load resistor inside the SWR meter (or back out the antenna again on the next bounce). At SWR ≤ 2:1, less than 11 % of your power is reflected — almost no one can tell the difference on the air. At 3:1 you've lost ~25 %; at 6:1, half your power.

SWR ≠ antenna efficiency. A 50 Ω dummy load shows a perfect 1:1 SWR and radiates nothing. SWR only tells you that your transmitter sees the right load — not that the energy is leaving the antenna instead of warming up loss in the coax.

4 · Impedance & matching

Why "50 Ω" everywhere, and what to do when your antenna isn't.

Antenna feedpoint impedance has two parts: Z = R ± jX — a real (resistive) part R, and an imaginary (reactive) part X. At resonance, X = 0 and only R matters. Common feedpoint resistances:

AntennaR at resonanceMatch needed?
½-wave dipole, free space~73 Ω1.46:1 SWR — usually no
½-wave dipole, low & horizontal~50–70 ΩOften direct-feed
¼-wave vertical, ideal ground~36 Ω~1.4:1 — fine; sometimes UNUN
End-fed half-wave (EFHW)~2 500–5 000 Ω49:1 UNUN required
Full-wave loop~100–120 Ω4:1 balun or λ/4 75 Ω matching
Folded dipole~300 Ω4:1 / 6:1 balun

Tools that change impedance

  • Balun — balanced ↔ unbalanced. Lets you feed a balanced antenna (dipole) with unbalanced coax, often with a turns-ratio that also transforms impedance (1:1, 4:1, 9:1).
  • UNUN — unbalanced ↔ unbalanced with a turns ratio. Common for end-feds (49:1, 64:1).
  • Antenna tuner / ATU — variable L/C network that matches what your radio sees to 50 Ω. Does not change what's at the antenna; it just hides the mismatch from the radio. Loss in the tuner + extra coax loss is real.
  • Stub matching — short open or shorted line section in parallel that cancels reactance.

5 · Tuning your antenna (the actual workflow)

"Tuning" can mean two completely different things. Know which one you're doing.

(a) Physical tuning — change the antenna

  1. Build it slightly longer than calculated (≈ 5 % long).
  2. Hang it in its final location, at final height. Connect feedline.
  3. Sweep with an antenna analyzer (NanoVNA, RigExpert, MFJ-259) and find the SWR minimum frequency.
  4. Calculate the trim: Δf / f₀ × total length. If the dip is below your target, the antenna is too long — shorten it. If above, you went too short and have to lengthen (or accept it).
  5. Trim a little (2–5 cm at a time on HF, mm on UHF), re-measure, repeat. Don't try to nail it in one cut.
trim_per_leg ≈ (current_f₀ − target_f₀) / target_f₀ × leg_length / 2

(b) Electrical tuning — match what you have

Use an antenna tuner (manual, automatic, or remote). Workflow:

  1. Reduce power to ~10 W (most tuners can't handle full power while searching).
  2. Key down with a continuous carrier (CW, FM, AM tune) — not SSB voice.
  3. Adjust until the radio sees ≤ 1.5:1.
  4. Increase power. Watch for arcing in cheap tuners.
A tuner at the radio with lossy coax still wastes most of your power as heat. A tuner placed at the antenna feedpoint (remote/auto) is dramatically more efficient on a mismatched antenna.

7 · Feedlines & loss

The cable between your radio and antenna is part of the antenna system. Cheap coax kills your signal.

Coax loss in dB grows with frequency, length, and SWR. A 30 m run of RG-58 at 432 MHz loses about 9 dB — that's 88 % of your power gone before it reaches the antenna, and 88 % of your weak-signal RX too.

8 · Ground & radials

For a vertical, the missing half of the antenna is the ground. If it's a bad ground, you have a bad antenna.

A ¼-wave vertical needs a counterpoise — ideally 16 to 32 elevated radials of ¼-λ each, or 32–64 ground-laid radials for a HF base. Fewer radials = lower efficiency and higher feedpoint impedance.

  • Elevated radials (≥ ¼-λ above ground): only need a few (4 is OK), behave predictably.
  • Ground-mounted radials: more is better; lay them on or just under soil; length doesn't have to be tuned (8–32 m random lengths fine).
  • RF ground ≠ DC/lightning ground. Two separate jobs. Bond all rod grounds together at one point per the NEC, but don't expect an 8-foot rod to be your "ground plane".
Common-mode current on the coax shield turns your feedline into an unintended part of the antenna. Add a current choke (1:1 air-wound or ferrite) at the feedpoint of any unbalanced feed to a balanced antenna.

10 · Field-tuning checklist

Print this. Tape it inside your toolbox.

  1. Cut the antenna 5 % long. You can shorten; you can't easily lengthen.
  2. Mount in final location and height before measuring. Even 1 m of extra height shifts resonance.
  3. Use an analyzer or VNA — not just an SWR meter — so you see resistance + reactance across the band.
  4. Find the SWR-minimum frequency. That is your current resonance.
  5. Compute trim with the formula in §5 and trim in small increments.
  6. Re-seal every connector. Water gets in. Water always gets in.
  7. Add a 1:1 current choke at the feedpoint.
  8. Document feedline length, type, antenna geometry, and the final SWR sweep. Photos count.
  9. Verify received performance — listen for known beacons, WSPR, ATIS — not just transmitted SWR.
If you only remember one thing: an antenna at height in the clear with a good feedline beats an exotic compact antenna near metal every time.
